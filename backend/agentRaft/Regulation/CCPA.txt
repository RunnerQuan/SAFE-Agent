CALIFORNIA PRIVACY PROTECTION AGENCY ENFORCEMENT DIVISIONENFORCEMENT ADVISORY NO. 2024-01APPLYING DATA MINIMIZATION TO CONSUMER REQUESTS
SUMMARYData minimization is a foundational principle in the CCPA. Many aspects of the CCPA’s implementing regulations underscore this principle. Businesses must apply 11 CCR § 7002(d)-in coordination with other applicable sections of the CCPA and its implementing regulations-for each purpose for which businesses collect, use, retain, and share personal information. Data minimization principles apply to the processing of consumers’ CCPA requests.
ENFORCEMENT OBSERVATIONSData minimization is a foundational principle in the CCPA. Businesses should apply this principle to every purpose for which they collect, use, retain, and share consumers’ personal information.Data minimization serves important functions: reducing the risk of unauthorized access to personal information (e.g., through data breaches), supporting good data governance, and enabling faster responses to consumers’ CCPA rights requests. Businesses should periodically assess their data practices from the perspective of data minimization.The Enforcement Division observes that certain businesses are asking consumers to provide excessive and unnecessary personal information in response to CCPA requests. Businesses must apply data minimization to all data practices, including when processing CCPA requests.

WHAT THE LAW AND REGULATIONS SAY ABOUT DATA MINIMIZATIONThe CCPA’s data minimization principle stems from the law’s intent that businesses collect personal information only to the extent relevant and limited to what is necessary for the stated purposes (Prop 24, § 3(B)(3)).CCPA states: “A business’ collection, use, retention, and sharing of a consumer’s personal information shall be reasonably necessary and proportionate to achieve the purposes for which the personal information was collected or processed, or for another disclosed purpose that is compatible with the context in which the personal information was collected, and not further processed in a manner that is incompatible with those purposes.” (Civil Code § 1798.100(c))Regulations explain: “[W]hether a business’s collection, use, retention, and/or sharing of a consumer’s personal information is reasonably necessary and proportionate to achieve the purpose identified … shall be based on the following:(1) The minimum personal information necessary to achieve the purpose (e.g., for online purchases: order, payment, shipping information, and email address for confirmation).(2) Possible negative impacts on consumers (e.g., collecting precise geolocation may reveal sensitive health information).(3) Existence of additional safeguards (e.g., encryption, automatic deletion within a specific time window).” (11 CCR § 7002(d))
Additional CCPA regulations reflecting data minimization:
Opt-out Preference Signals: “The business shall not require a consumer to provide additional information beyond what is necessary to send the signal.” (11 CCR § 7025(c)(2))
Requests to Opt-out of Sale/Sharing: “A business shall not require a consumer submitting a request to opt-out of sale/sharing to create an account or provide additional information beyond what is necessary to direct the business not to sell or share the consumer’s personal information.” (11 CCR § 7026(c))
Requests to Limit Use and Disclosure of Sensitive Personal Information: “A business shall not require a consumer submitting a request to limit to create an account or provide additional information beyond what is necessary to direct the business to limit the use or disclosure of the consumer’s sensitive personal information.” (11 CCR § 7027(d))
General Rules Regarding Verification:
(1) “Whenever feasible, match the identifying information provided by the consumer to the personal information of the consumer already maintained by the business, or use a third-party identity verification service that complies with this section.
(2) Avoid collecting the types of personal information identified in Civil Code section 1798.81.5, subdivision (d) [e.g., Social Security number, driver’s license number, financial account numbers, unique biometric data], unless necessary for verification….” (11 CCR § 7060(c))
“A business shall generally avoid requesting additional information from the consumer for purposes of verification. If verification via existing information is not feasible, additional information may be requested solely for verification, security, or fraud-prevention. Such new information shall be deleted as soon as practical after processing the request, except as required by section 7101.” (11 CCR § 7060(d))
FACTUAL SCENARIOSSCENARIO ONE: RESPONDING TO A REQUEST TO OPT-OUT OF SALE/SHARINGBusiness A (covered by CCPA) receives consumer requests to opt-out of sale/sharing of personal information.Relevant rules: Civil Code §§ 1798.120, 1798.135; 11 CCR §§ 7025, 7026, 7060(b).Key requirement: “A business shall not require a consumer to verify their identity to make a request to opt-out of sale/sharing or to make a request to limit. A business may ask for information necessary to complete the request, but it shall not be burdensome (e.g., may ask for name, but not require a photo with driver’s license).” (11 CCR § 7060(b))Business A should ask:
What is the minimum personal information necessary to honor the opt-out request?
Is additional information needed beyond what is already maintained?
What are the possible negative impacts of collecting additional information?
Can additional safeguards address these negative impacts?
Examples:
If Business A sells/shares only online activities for cross-context behavioral advertising, no additional information (e.g., name, email) is needed for opt-out via preference signals.
If Business A sells/shares consumer profiles (online activity + purchasing history), consumers may need to provide limited identifying information, but unrelated information (e.g., driver’s license) is excessive.
SCENARIO TWO: VERIFICATION OF A CONSUMER’S IDENTITYBusiness B (covered by CCPA) receives requests to delete personal information from non-accountholders (has consumer names and email addresses on file).Relevant rules: 11 CCR § 7060(a) (reasonable verification method), 11 CCR § 7062 (Verification for Non-Accountholders), 11 CCR § 7061 (Verification for Password-Protected Accounts).Business B should ask:
What is the minimum personal information necessary for identity verification?
Is additional information needed beyond what is already maintained?
What are the possible negative impacts of collecting/using the information?
Can additional safeguards address these negative impacts?
Additional questions for Business B:
For name + email deletion: What degree of verification certainty (reasonable/reasonably high) is needed? How sensitive is the information, and what is the risk of unauthorized deletion? Can email alone be relied on, or is driver’s license/Social Security number necessary (disproportionate/excessive)?
For accounts with photos/documents (accessed via email + password + code): Are the files sensitive enough to warrant stricter verification? Can email be spoofed? Is driver’s license information necessary (disproportionate/excessive)? What are the risks of collecting driver’s license numbers (e.g., breach)? Can code request/confirmation be used for reauthentication?
